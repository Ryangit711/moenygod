# Global Monetary Plumbing: How Money Enters the System (Country-Agnostic)

> **Core Principle**: Central bank creates reserves → commercial banks issue credit → that credit becomes the money stock. No nation names. Only functions.

---

## I. The Two-Layer Engine (Universal)

| Layer | Actor | Action | Result |
|-------|-------|--------|--------|
| **Layer 1** | Central Bank | Issues **reserves** (liabilities of the central bank) by purchasing assets or lending to commercial banks | Reserves appear on commercial banks' asset side; central bank balance sheet expands |
| **Layer 2** | Commercial Banks | Issue **debt contracts** (loans, mortgages, credit lines) to non-bank entities, crediting deposit accounts | New deposits = new broad money (M2/M3). Reserves stay inside the banking system; deposits circulate in the real economy |

> **Key identity**: `Δ Broad Money ≈ Δ Commercial Bank Credit` (modulo leakages: currency drain, central bank reverse operations, cross-border flows).

---

## II. Step-by-Step: From Reserve Creation to Deposit Circulation

1. **Central Bank Operation**
   - Asset purchase (government bonds, mortgage-backed securities, corporate paper, FX) **OR** lending facility (discount window, repo, standing facility).
   - Pays by crediting the selling bank's **reserve account** at the central bank.
   - Central bank balance sheet: **Assets ↑ | Reserves (liability) ↑**.

2. **Commercial Bank Response**
   - Holds new reserves. Can now meet regulatory liquidity ratios (LCR, reserve requirements) more easily.
   - Extends a new loan: creates a **loan asset** and a **deposit liability** simultaneously (double-entry).
   - No prior reserve needed for the *individual* loan; system-wide reserve adequacy matters.

3. **Deposit Enters Economy**
   - Borrower spends the deposit → recipient's bank receives reserves via interbank settlement.
   - Reserves **shuffle** between banks; total system reserves unchanged (unless central bank drains/injects).
   - Deposit velocity × stock = nominal spending (NGDP proxy).

4. **Leakages & Drains**
   - **Currency withdrawal**: public converts deposits to cash → reserves ↓, cash in circulation ↑.
   - **Central bank reverse repo / asset sales / rate hikes**: reserves ↓.
   - **Cross-border outflows**: reserves leave the domestic system (FX intervention sterilization).
   - **Loan repayment / write-offs**: deposits destroyed, broad money ↓.

5. **Feedback to Central Bank**
   - Central bank watches: inflation, credit growth, asset prices, exchange rate.
   - Adjusts policy rate / balance sheet → influences cost & availability of reserves → feeds back to step 1.

## III. Country-Agnostic Taxonomy of Major Economies

For each economy below, the **structure is identical**; only scale, institutional labels, and policy tools differ.

| Economy Tier | Central Bank Function | Commercial Bank System | Distinctive Plumbing Feature |
|--------------|----------------------|------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Reserve-Currency Issuer (Tier 1)** | Issues the **global reserve asset**; its reserves are held worldwide. Operates swap lines to other central banks. | Deep, globally connected; large USD/EUR/GBP/JPY/CHF funding markets. | **Exorbitant privilege**: can export its currency; foreign demand for its reserves dampens domestic inflation from money growth. |
| **Major Non-Reserve Advanced (Tier 2)** | Issues a **freely floated, internationally used** currency. Holds own FX reserves. | Well-capitalized; significant domestic bond markets. | **Swap-line access** to Tier 1; currency acts as regional anchor. |
| **Emerging Market with Managed Float (Tier 3)** | Actively manages FX rate; accumulates large FX reserves. Sterilizes inflows via domestic bond sales / reserve requirements. | Often state-influenced; dollarized loan books common. | **Dual anchor**: domestic inflation target + exchange-rate stability. Sterilization creates domestic interest-rate distortion. |
| **Commodity-Exporter (Tier 4)** | Revenue heavily tied to commodity price (oil, gas, metals, ag). Builds sovereign wealth funds. | Banking sector linked to commodity cycles; credit booms/busts track export revenue. | **Terms-of-trade channel**: export windfall → FX reserves ↑ → domestic liquidity ↑ unless sterilized. |
| **Currency-Board / Hard Peg (Tier 5)** | No independent monetary policy; issues domestic currency **only** against foreign reserve asset (1:1 or fixed ratio). | Credit growth strictly limited by reserve backing. | **Automatic adjustment**: money supply = FX reserves × fixed multiplier. No lender-of-last-resort in domestic currency. |
| **Dollarized / Euroized (Tier 6)** | No domestic central bank money creation; uses foreign currency as legal tender. | Banks are effectively branches of foreign system; liquidity depends on foreign central bank. | **Imported monetary policy**; domestic credit = function of foreign reserves + cross-border flows. |

> **Universal Rule**: Every tier follows *Layer 1 → Layer 2* mechanics. Differences are **constraints** (FX regime, reserve currency status, commodity dependence), not **mechanics**.

## IV. Global Flow Map: How a Reserve Injection in One Tier Ripples Outward

```
[Tier 1 Central Bank expands reserves]
        │
        ├─→ Domestic commercial banks lend more → domestic deposits ↑ → domestic demand ↑
        │
        ├─→ Swap lines / FX reserves flow to Tier 2 & 3 central banks
        │       └─→ Their commercial banks get cheaper FX funding → credit ↑ in those economies
        │
        ├─→ Portfolio rebalancing: global investors sell Tier 1 bonds → buy Tier 2/3/4 assets
        │       └─→ Asset prices ↑, yields ↓ abroad → easier financial conditions globally
        │
        ├─→ Commodity pricing (oil, gold, food) denominated in Tier 1 currency → price ↑
        │       └─→ Tier 4 export revenue ↑ → their FX reserves ↑ → domestic liquidity ↑
        │
        └─→ Exchange-rate pressure on Tier 3/5/6
                5/6
                └─→ They intervene (sell FX reserves, raise rates, tighten macroprudential)
                        └─→ Their domestic reserves ↓ or rates ↑ → credit growth slows
```

**Feedback Loop**: Slower credit in Tier 3-6 → weaker import demand → Tier 1 export growth slows → Tier 1 central bank may pause/reverse.

## V. Oikonomia Lens: Household Management at Planetary Scale

| Oikonomia Question | Plumbing Answer |
|--------------------|-----------------|
| **Who manages the household?** | Central banks (Tier 1 collectively, Tier 2-6 nationally) + commercial bank credit committees. |
| **For whose benefit?** | Tier 1: global asset holders (disproportionately). Tier 2-4: domestic elites + export sectors. Tier 5-6: peg credibility > domestic welfare. |
| **What is the "store of value"?** | Tier 1 reserves / sovereign debt (global), Tier 2-4 local currency deposits + FX, Tier 5-6 foreign currency cash. |
| **What is the "medium of exchange"?** | Commercial bank deposits (everywhere), cash (declining), stablecoins (emerging). |
| **Where does the surplus go?** | Tier 1: financial assets (equities, real estate, private equity). Tier 2-4: domestic property, sovereign wealth funds, FX reserves. Tier 5-6: foreign bank deposits. |

> **Insight**: The *oikonomia* (household management) is **fragmented** — no single manager. Each tier manages its own sub-household; spillovers are externalities, not coordinated policy.

## VIII. Quick-Reference Cheat Sheet (One Page)

| Concept | One-Liner |
|---------|-----------|
| **Reserves** | Central bank liabilities; only banks hold them; used for interbank settlement. |
| **Broad Money (M2/M3)** | Commercial bank deposits + currency; created when banks lend. |
| **Credit Creation** | Loan asset + deposit liability = new money; no prior reserve needed *ex ante*. |
| **Policy Rate** | Price of reserves (interbank); influences banks' marginal funding cost. |
| **QE / Asset Purchase** | Central bank swaps reserves for long-term assets → lowers term premium, signals rates. |
| **Swap Line** | Tier 1 central bank lends its currency to another central bank → relieves FX funding stress. |
| **Sterilization** | Central bank offsets FX inflow by selling domestic bonds / raising reserve requirements → drains reserves. |
| **Dollar Milkshake** | Global demand for USD reserves sucks liquidity from periphery → crisis → Fed eases → repeat. |
| **Fiscal-Monetary Loop** | Treasury issues debt → central bank buys (directly or via banks) → reserves ↑ → deposits ↑ → spending ↑. |
| **Shadow Banking** | Non-bank credit intermediation (MMFs, repo, FX swaps) — creates *dollar claims* without reserves. |

## X. Actionable Takeaway for the Trader

1. **Watch Tier 1 central bank balance sheets weekly** (Fed, ECB, BoJ, BoE, SNB, PBOC). Net change = global reserve impulse.
2. **Track swap-line usage & FX swap basis** — signals Tier 2/3 dollar funding stress.
3. **Monitor Tier 4 commodity revenue → FX reserve accrual → domestic credit impulse** (e.g., Canada oil, Australia iron ore, Brazil soy).
4. **Read Tier 3 sterilization signals** (reverse repo volumes, reserve requirement changes) — they reveal *intent* to slow credit.
5. **Position in the pipe**: long assets where reserves/credit expand; short where they contract. Use futures/forex for direct expression.
